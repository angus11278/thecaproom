The 10-second read

How to read the sonar

  1. Each ring is a season, 2021 in the centre out to 2025. Each of the 18 spokes is a club.
  2. The ring itself is zero luck. A dot sitting exactly on its ring means the club won precisely what the betting market expected. A teal dot pushed outside the ring won more than the money predicted. A rose dot pulled inside won less.
  3. Distance is the size of the luck, measured in wins. Collingwood 2022 sits 5.1 wins outside its ring. West Coast 2025 sits 4.4 wins inside.
  4. The tell: follow any club's line through its five dots. Almost nobody stays outside. That wandering is the whole finding: market-beating does not repeat, so it is not a skill.

Market findings · six seasons audited

What the money got wrong

The comeback premium
15.3% vs 20.0% priced

They are not coming back

593 half-time situations between $3 and $15. The market prices the comeback at one in five. It lands one in six and a half. Backing every comeback lost 23.7 per cent flat stake over six seasons.

The crowd experiment
+0.5 then +3.2 pts

Home advantage left with the crowds. The market noticed. Then forgot.

In the COVID seasons, home teams beat their price by half a point: empty grounds, priced almost perfectly. Since the crowds returned, home teams beat their price by 3.2 points. The gap has not closed.

Two codes, opposite errors
19.8% vs 14.4% priced

NRL longshots are underbet

Rugby league outsiders win more than their price says. Hot favourites win less. The AFL market shows no such bias over six seasons. Same country, same punters, opposite mistakes.

The player layer · v0.1 · validation season 2020

Production, not points

Each club's offensive, defensive and midfield output is valued at team level, then split among players by their share of the work. Creators get paid, not just finishers. Validation: 0.69 rank correlation with Brownlow votes, which never touch the model. The 2021-25 refresh and the salary layer land at trade period.

HOW PLAYER VALUE WORKS, IN ONE PICTURE

"Brownlow held out", in plain speech

  1. The model never sees a Brownlow vote. It ranks every player purely on the production maths above.
  2. Then we open the sealed envelope. After the rankings are locked, we compare them against ten years of umpire votes: judges who watched every game and owe our model nothing.
  3. Agreement: 0.69 out of 1. Two independent systems, one made of regressions and one made of umpires, largely agree on who mattered. If we had let the votes into the model, this test would prove nothing. Keeping them out is what makes it proof.

Why not just use the votes?

  1. Votes are three names a night. Our number covers all 44 players on the park, every week, including the ones umpires never poll: the lockdown defender, the third tall, the second ruck.
  2. Votes cannot be priced. Value per contract dollar needs a continuous production measure for every listed player. That is the layer the salary model attaches to at trade period.

The Luck Ledger · No. 1

Nineteen coin flips: the Collingwood myth, audited

Everyone in football believes the same thing about Collingwood: they know how to win the close ones. We took every AFL match-odds market on the exchange from 2021 to 2025 and asked the one referee that cannot barrack.

31 wins from 46 close games, 67 per cent, in games the market priced at 53.5 per cent. In the peak years of 2022-23: 19 from 23. Eighty-three per cent, in coin flips. The league baseline over 560 close games is a perfect 50.0 against an expected 50.0.

So the legend is real. Now read the ledger: minus 3.4, plus 5.1, plus 2.8, plus 0.5, plus 0.6. The magic arrives in 2022, peaks, and drains to zero. That is the shape of luck, not skill. Across the whole competition, beating the market one year predicts nothing about the next. Skill compounds. Luck mean-reverts. This curve mean-reverted.

Methodology · public summary

Built to be attacked

The full specification, estimation procedures, allocation weights and salary-model priors are proprietary. What we publish is everything you need to trust the output, and everything you need to try to break it.

Output: expected wins

The closing exchange price, margin removed, is the best available estimate of true strength: calibrated within three points everywhere, with residuals that show zero persistence and zero split-half reliability. We measure clubs against it, not against the ladder.

The luck firewall

Actual minus expected wins is its own published column and never enters a skill claim. It sums to zero by construction and behaves statistically like noise, because it is noise.

Inputs: value per dollar

Player production is valued in three pools and allocated by share of work. Salaries are modelled in ranges and reconciled to the AFL's officially published club payment totals, so club-level figures hold even where individual estimates carry bands.

QA, in public

Every figure passes independent re-derivation, out-of-sample splits, reliability testing and invariant checks. Findings that fail are published as kills in the revision log. This page already carries two: a dead longshot bias and a corrected draw-settlement bug.
