# THE LUCK LEDGER #1
## Nineteen coin flips: the Collingwood myth, audited

*The Cap Room · Launch series · All figures from Betfair exchange closing prices, 2021–2025, vig-removed. Methodology and code published.*

---

Everyone in football believes the same thing about Collingwood: they know how to win the close ones. Craig McRae's "we've been here before" composure. The siren-edge escapes. A premiership in 2023 built on a string of nail-biters. It is the most repeated claim in the modern game, and to our knowledge nobody has ever audited it against the one referee that can't barrack — the market.

So we did. We took every AFL match-odds market on the Betfair exchange from 2021 to 2025, stripped the bookmaker's margin from the closing prices, and asked a simple question: in games decided by under two goals, how often did Collingwood win, and how often did the money say they should?

**The answer: 31 wins from 46 close games — 67% — when the market priced them at 53.5%.** In the legend's peak years of 2022–23, it was 19 wins from 23. Eighty-three per cent, in games the market rated a coin flip.

That is not a normal record. The league-wide baseline over the same five seasons is exactly what theory predicts: in 560 close games, teams won exactly 50.0% of the ones the market gave them a 50.0% chance in — a perfect coin-flip ledger. Everyone else's close games are coin flips. Collingwood's, for two seasons, were not.

So the legend is real. The question is what it was made of.

### The ledger

Here is Collingwood's full account with the market, season by season — actual wins minus the wins the closing prices expected:

| Season | Expected wins | Actual | Ledger |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2021 | 9.4 | 6 | **−3.4** |
| 2022 | 10.9 | 16 | **+5.1** |
| 2023 | 15.2 | 18 | **+2.8** |
| 2024 | 12.5 | 13 | +0.5 |
| 2025 | 15.4 | 16 | +0.6 |

Read it like a bank statement. In 2021 — same list core, one year before the magic — Collingwood ran three and a half wins *below* expectation. Then two extraordinary surplus years. Then the balance drains: +0.5, +0.6 — back to zero.

If close-game mastery were a skill — a coaching system, a cultural asset, something a club owns — it should persist. We tested that across the whole competition: six seasons, every club, 72 club-season pairs, with drawn games settled at half a win exactly as the exchange settles them. The year-to-year correlation of over-performance against the market is **−0.04**. Statistically, nothing. Clubs that beat the market's expectations one year are no more likely than anyone else to do it the next. We even split each club's season in half and checked whether over-performance in a team's even-numbered games predicted its odd-numbered games. It doesn't.

The market, it turns out, already prices everything a club genuinely owns — the list, the coach, the system, the fixture. What's left over is the residue the price couldn't know in advance. There is a word for that residue.

### What this doesn't mean

It doesn't mean the 2023 premiership is diminished — flags are won by being good enough to be in coin flips that matter, and Collingwood's underlying strength was real and the market rated it (15.2 expected wins, second in the competition). It doesn't mean McRae's composure is fiction; it means its effect was already in the price. And one honesty note our own QA process demands: we audited Collingwood *because* of the legend, which flatters any significance test (the raw probability of their close-game run is p = 0.003 — but you don't get to pick the streak after watching it). The stronger evidence is the persistence result, and the ledger's own shape: the magic arrives in 2022, peaks in 2023, and is gone by 2025. Skill compounds. Luck mean-reverts. This curve mean-reverted.

### Why we publish this

Because the most expensive mistakes in football are made by mistaking the ledger for the list. Clubs re-sign veterans off the back of surplus years. Coaches are sacked in deficit years. Members renew their faith, and their money, on narratives the closing price had already discounted. The Luck Ledger will run for every club, every season, alongside our efficiency ratings — which measure the thing that *does* persist: how much market-rated strength a list buys per cap dollar.

The legend was real. It just wasn't a skill. Nineteen coin flips came up black, and football called it culture.
